If you are standing in the garden aisle right now trying to decide between a bag of Barricade and a bag of Dimension, you are asking the right question. Prodiamine and dithiopyr are the two most effective pre-emergent active ingredients available to homeowners, and they are not interchangeable in every situation. Choosing the wrong one for your timing and location is the most common reason pre-emergent programs fail in year two and three.

Here is the plain-language breakdown of how each one works, where each wins, and exactly which one to reach for depending on your situation right now.

How Pre-Emergents Actually Work

Pre-emergent herbicides do not prevent crabgrass seeds from sprouting. The seed germinates normally underground — then the emerging seedling contacts the chemical barrier in the top layer of soil and dies before it reaches sunlight. Miss the window, and there is no barrier.

Crabgrass seeds germinate when soil temperatures at the 1–2 inch depth reach a consistent 55°F for three or more days. In the Southeast and transition zone, that window typically opens in late February through March. In the Midwest and Northeast, it runs from mid-April through mid-May depending on the year. To check your local soil temps right now, use your state's cooperative extension soil temperature map or the GreenCast soil temperature tool.

💡 Phenological indicator: Forsythia bloom is a widely used field signal for early pre-emergent timing. Lilac bloom indicates you are approaching the outer edge of your window. Both correlate closely with growing degree days used by university extension models.

Prodiamine: The Early-Season Workhorse

Prodiamine is the active ingredient in Barricade and several generic WDG (water-dispersible granule) products. It works by inhibiting cell division in the shoot and root of emerging seedlings. It is strictly a pre-emergent — once crabgrass has broken the soil surface, prodiamine has no effect on it.

Its major advantage is residual length. Applied at the full spring rate, a single prodiamine application can maintain an effective soil barrier for up to six months, meaning one application in late March or early April can carry protection through late summer when late-season crabgrass germination would otherwise fill in thin spots.

Prodiamine also binds tightly to soil particles and does not leach readily with heavy rain — a meaningful advantage in spring-wet regions like the mid-Atlantic, Midwest, and Pacific Northwest, where Dithiopyr's higher water solubility can reduce efficacy after a heavy rain event within the first two weeks of application.

For homeowners who prefer a liquid application with a pump sprayer, the most cost-efficient prodiamine product on the market is Prodiamine 65 WDG, which is the pure active ingredient in a wettable powder form. Mixed at the label-recommended rate of 0.5–0.75 oz per 1,000 sq ft for spring applications on established turf, a 5 lb container treats 40,000–60,000 sq ft — far cheaper per square foot than any consumer bag. If you are comfortable mixing product and using a pump sprayer, find Prodiamine 65 WDG on Amazon and check current sizing options.

Dithiopyr: The Forgiving Option With a Post-Emergent Bonus

Dithiopyr is the active ingredient in Dimension. It works similarly to prodiamine as a pre-emergent, but it has one property prodiamine does not: early post-emergent activity on crabgrass that has already emerged, provided the plant is still at or before the 1–2 tiller stage (roughly 2–3 weeks old and still very small).

This makes dithiopyr the right call for two specific situations: you think you may be applying slightly late, or you want a second application later in spring to extend coverage into the summer germination window. The residual protection runs 4–5 months versus 6 months for prodiamine, so a second application in late May or early June using dithiopyr extends your protection and catches any late germinators that slipped through the first round.

Dithiopyr is also clear in color with minimal odor, which matters if you are applying near concrete, pavers, or light-colored surfaces where prodiamine's yellow tint can leave visible residue on equipment and hard surfaces.

The most accessible granular dithiopyr format for homeowners is a 0.10% dithiopyr fertilizer carrier. The Andersons Barricade Professional-Grade pre-emergent granular (dithiopyr formulation) is widely available and sized for 10,000–15,000 sq ft coverage. If your spring has been warm and you are reading this in late April or early May, a dithiopyr granular on Amazon is the correct choice over prodiamine at this point in the season.

⚠️ Seeding conflict: Both prodiamine and dithiopyr will prevent your grass seed from germinating just as effectively as they prevent crabgrass. If you plan to overseed this fall, a spring prodiamine application at full rate can leave enough residual to interfere with September seeding. For fall overseeding lawns, use the lower end of the label rate range in spring, or switch to a mesotrione-based pre-emergent (like Tenacity) which is seed-safe.

The Split-Application Strategy That Professionals Use

The most reliable pre-emergent program is not a single application of one product — it is two applications using both. Apply prodiamine first when soil temps approach 50–52°F, then follow with a dithiopyr application 6–8 weeks later, just before soil temps push past 65°F. The dithiopyr second round extends your coverage window into summer, catches any seeds that germinated after the first round's residual began fading, and adds the early post-emergent backstop for anything that snuck through.

You will also need a calibrated soil thermometer to time both applications accurately — air temperature is not soil temperature, and a warm week in March does not mean the ground is ready. Push the probe 2 inches into the soil in the morning (the coldest reading of the day) and track consecutive readings before pulling the trigger.

What to Do If You Have Already Missed the Window

If soil temperatures in your area have already been above 55°F for more than a week, crabgrass seeds have started germinating. A prodiamine application right now will still prevent late-season germinators (crabgrass germinates in waves through early July), but it will not kill seedlings already in the ground. This is where dithiopyr earns its keep: if plants are still very small — fewer than three leaves, no tillers visible — dithiopyr applied at the full label rate may still knock them out.

For crabgrass plants that are beyond the tiller stage, you need a post-emergent option. Quinclorac-based products (active ingredient in Drive XLR8 and several generic versions) are the most effective post-emergent crabgrass killers for established cool-season lawns. Apply to actively growing plants, keep foot traffic off for 24 hours, and do not mow for 2 days before or after application. Find quinclorac post-emergent on Amazon if you are past the pre-emergent window entirely.

💡 Mowing height matters more than most people realize: University of Nebraska research found that raising mowing height from 1.5 inches to 2.5 inches reduced crabgrass infestation from 80% down to less than 15% over time — with no chemical applied. Pre-emergent is a tool; a dense, tall canopy is your best long-term defense.

Grass Type Considerations

Both prodiamine and dithiopyr are labeled safe for most established warm- and cool-season turfgrasses. The exception to watch is Bermuda: prodiamine can act as a mild root pruner on Bermuda and slightly slow lateral spread, which matters if you are trying to fill in a thin Bermuda lawn after winter. If you have Bermuda and want aggressive spring fill-in, dithiopyr is the safer pre-emergent choice this season.

St. Augustine, Zoysia, Kentucky Bluegrass, Tall Fescue, and Perennial Rye are all tolerant of both active ingredients at standard homeowner label rates. Always verify your specific grass type on the product label before applying — label language supersedes any general guidance.

📋 Which One to Buy Right Now

  • Soil temps below 52°F and you plan to overseed in fall: Prodiamine WDG at the lower end of the label rate range
  • Soil temps at 52–58°F and you are right at the window: Either works — dithiopyr granular if you want the post-emergent safety net
  • Soil temps above 58°F or you think you may be late: Dithiopyr at full label rate, applied immediately
  • Second application 6–8 weeks after first: Always use dithiopyr to extend coverage and add post-emergent activity
  • No pre-emergent this year, crabgrass already visible: Quinclorac post-emergent, not a pre-emergent

Pre-emergents do not substitute for a healthy lawn. Thin, compacted turf with bare spots will see crabgrass push through the barrier no matter what chemical you use. The barrier only works on germinating seeds — and where your lawn is dense and mowed at 3–3.5 inches, fewer seeds get the light and heat they need to germinate in the first place. Fix the soil, mow high, and let the chemistry handle the seeds that get past your turf's natural defense.
