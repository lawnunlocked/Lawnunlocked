First: Check Your Lease

Before buying a single piece of lawn equipment, pull out your lease and look for language about lawn maintenance. Responsibilities vary widely — some landlords handle all exterior maintenance, some split it, and some make lawn care entirely the tenant's responsibility. Common lease language includes phrases like "tenant shall maintain lawn in neat condition" or "landlord responsible for landscaping."

  • If your lease says you're responsible for lawn care, you're on the hook for mowing, edging, and basic upkeep
  • Major issues — dead grass from a disease, irrigation system repairs, tree removal — are almost always the landlord's responsibility regardless of what the lease says about routine maintenance
  • If it's unclear, email your landlord and ask in writing so you have documentation
💡 Document the lawn's condition with photos when you move in. This protects your security deposit if a landlord tries to charge you for pre-existing lawn damage at move-out.

What Renters Are Typically Responsible For

  • Mowing — keeping grass at a reasonable height (usually under 6 inches per local ordinances)
  • Edging — keeping edges along sidewalks and driveways tidy
  • Watering — if there's no irrigation system, basic watering during dry spells
  • Weeding — pulling obvious weeds in beds if specified in the lease
  • Leaves — raking and bagging in fall in many leases

What Landlords Are Typically Responsible For

  • Irrigation system maintenance and repairs
  • Tree trimming and removal
  • Major lawn renovation or reseeding
  • Pest and disease treatment for established infestations
  • Any structural issues (retaining walls, drainage problems)
⚠️ Don't do major lawn work — fertilizing, aerating, overseeding — without checking with your landlord first. Some landlords have lawn care contracts with professional services and don't want tenants interfering. Others will appreciate the initiative. Ask first.

The Renter's Minimal Equipment List

The key for renters is buying equipment that's portable, storable in a small space, and useful at your next place. Avoid gas equipment if you can — battery-powered tools require no fuel storage and are easy to transport.

  • Cordless battery lawn mower — foldable, fits in a closet, no gas, moves with you. For yards under 1/4 acre a 40V or 60V battery mower handles everything.
  • Battery string trimmer — handles edges, around trees, and anywhere the mower can't reach. Buy the same brand as your mower to share batteries.
  • Portable oscillating sprinkler — attaches to any hose, covers up to 4,000 sq ft, packs flat. Essential if there's no irrigation system.
  • Handheld fertilizer spreader — small, cheap, does the job for a rental yard. No need for a full broadcast spreader you'll have to store.
  • Hose-end sprayer — for liquid fertilizer, weed killer, or fungicide. Attaches to your garden hose, no pump required.

Fertilizing as a Renter: Keep It Simple

Unless your landlord specifically asks you not to, a light fertilizer application in spring and fall does nothing but help the lawn. Stick to a balanced slow-release granular — it's forgiving, hard to over-apply, and won't create problems. Avoid heavy nitrogen applications that push excessive growth you'll just have to mow more often.

When to Call the Landlord Instead of Fixing It Yourself

  • Large dead patches that appeared suddenly — could be a disease, grub infestation, or irrigation failure
  • Standing water that doesn't drain within 24 hours — drainage issue, not a lawn care issue
  • Mushrooms appearing in a circle (fairy rings) — soil fungal issue that requires professional treatment
  • Mole or vole tunneling — pest control, not routine maintenance

The Complete Renter Lawn Kit

  • Cordless battery mower — portable, no gas, closet-storable
  • Battery string trimmer — same battery platform as mower
  • Portable sprinkler — hose-connected, packs flat
  • Handheld spreader — for fertilizer and seed
  • Hose-end sprayer — for liquid applications
