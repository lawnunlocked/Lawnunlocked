Bermuda grass is the most aggressive warm-season turf grass in North America — when managed correctly, it fills in bare spots on its own, outcompetes most weeds through sheer density, and can achieve a finish that rivals professional sports fields. When managed incorrectly, it turns thin, weedy, and difficult to recover.

The difference between a spectacular Bermuda lawn and a struggling one usually comes down to three factors: mowing at the right height with the right equipment, fertilizing in the right season at the right rate, and applying pre-emergent herbicide before the crabgrass window opens each spring. This guide covers all three in detail.

Bermuda Grass Profile

Bermuda grass (Cynodon dactylon) is native to Africa and thrives in hot, humid climates across the American South, Southwest, and transition zone. It spreads aggressively through both above-ground stolons and below-ground rhizomes, which gives it exceptional recovery ability from damage but also makes it invasive in garden beds and adjacent properties.

Key characteristics that drive all care decisions:

  • Growth habit: Active growth from late spring through early fall; full dormancy when soil temps drop below 50°F
  • Shade tolerance: Very low — requires at least 6 hours of direct sun for adequate density
  • Drought tolerance: Excellent — goes dormant rather than dying under extended drought
  • Traffic tolerance: Outstanding — recovers rapidly from wear and compaction
  • Thatch tendency: High — requires annual scalping and periodic dethatching

Mowing: Height, Frequency, and Reel vs. Rotary

Mowing is where most Bermuda owners make their biggest mistake. Bermuda is a low-growing grass that performs best at heights that most rotary mowers cannot cleanly achieve. The closer you mow Bermuda (within reason), the denser and more weed-resistant it becomes.

Residential lawn goal: 1–1.5 inches. This is achievable with a rotary mower set to its lowest setting on most units, but produces a noticeably better result with a reel mower's scissor cut.

Showpiece or golf-course goal: 0.5–1 inch. At this height, a reel mower is essential. Rotary mowers at this height tear and bruise the grass blade rather than cutting cleanly, producing a brown cast and increasing disease entry.

The Fiskars StaySharp Max Reel Mower cuts cleanly at 1–4 inches and is the right tool for most serious Bermuda homeowners. For the obsessive golf-course finish at sub-inch heights, a powered gas reel mower (sold direct from McLane or Toro, not available on Amazon) is required.

Mowing frequency during peak growing season (June–August): Every 5–7 days. Never remove more than one-third of the blade height in a single cutting — removing more triggers a stress response that causes the lawn to shift resources to blade regrowth rather than lateral spread and root development.

Spring scalping: Before green-up in early spring (soil temps approaching 55°F), scalp the lawn aggressively — mow as low as your mower goes and bag the clippings. This removes the dead dormant material that accumulated over winter, allows sunlight to reach the soil and accelerate spring green-up, and removes habitat for disease spores. This is one of the most impactful single practices for a Bermuda lawn and is widely skipped by homeowners unfamiliar with warm-season grass management.

💡 Dull mower blades on Bermuda cut at low heights cause significant visible damage — the grass blade has a brownish cast after mowing that should not be confused with drought stress. Sharpen rotary blades every 25–30 hours of use. A reel mower's blades should be backlapped annually.

Fertilizing Schedule

Bermuda is a heavy nitrogen feeder during its active growing season. The fertilizing schedule should track soil temperature, not calendar date, because Bermuda roots only actively absorb nutrients when soil is warm enough for active growth.

  • First application: When soil temps consistently exceed 65°F (typically late April–May depending on region). A balanced fertilizer like The Andersons PGF Complete 16-4-8 feeds for 6–8 weeks and is appropriate for this timing.
  • Summer applications: Every 6–8 weeks through the growing season. In peak summer heat (July–August), use slow-release nitrogen only. Milorganite is the safest summer fertilizer for Bermuda — organic-based, impossible to burn, and effective at building overall soil health.
  • Last application: 6–8 weeks before the first expected frost in your area. Late fertilization pushes succulent growth that is damaged by frost and can weaken winter hardiness.
  • Do not fertilize during dormancy. November through March, Bermuda roots are not actively absorbing nutrients. Fertilizer applied during dormancy is wasted and contributes to nutrient runoff.

Weed Control Calendar

Bermuda's aggressive spreading habit makes it naturally weed-suppressive at proper density and mowing height — but it requires external help in the spring and fall when weed pressure peaks and the grass is either emerging from dormancy or declining toward it.

Pre-emergent (spring): Apply Prodiamine 65 WDG before soil temperatures hit 55°F. This is the most important weed control step of the year. A single application delivers 5–6 months of crabgrass and annual weed protection through the summer. Apply before the spring scalping if possible, or immediately after.

Post-emergent (summer): Spot-treat visible broadleaf weeds with a selective post-emergent labeled safe for Bermuda. Most MSMA-based herbicides are highly effective on Bermuda-tolerant applications. Avoid blanket applications during peak heat stress (soil above 90°F surface temperature).

Pre-emergent (fall): A second pre-emergent application in early fall prevents winter annual weeds (annual bluegrass, henbit, chickweed) from establishing during Bermuda's dormant period.

Watering Correctly

Bermuda is one of the most drought-tolerant turf grasses available — it goes dormant rather than dying under extended dry conditions, and greens back up quickly once moisture returns. This means over-watering is a more common problem than under-watering for established Bermuda lawns.

Established Bermuda needs approximately 1–1.25 inches of water per week during the growing season, either from rainfall or irrigation. Water deeply twice per week rather than shallowly daily — this drives root depth and builds drought resistance. A rain gauge tells you exactly how much supplemental irrigation you need after accounting for rainfall.

New Bermuda (seeded, sprigged, or sodded within the past 60 days) requires more frequent watering — daily or twice daily during establishment until root depth reaches 2–3 inches.

Seasonal Care Overview

🌸 Spring

  • Scalp lawn before green-up
  • Apply pre-emergent (Prodiamine)
  • First fertilizer application at 65°F soil
  • Soil test if not done recently

☀️ Summer

  • Mow every 5–7 days at target height
  • Continue fertilizing every 6–8 weeks
  • Water 1–1.25" per week
  • Spot-treat weeds as needed

🍂 Fall

  • Last fertilizer 6–8 weeks before frost
  • Apply fall pre-emergent
  • Core aerate in early fall
  • Begin reducing irrigation

❄️ Winter

  • No fertilizing during dormancy
  • No mowing needed
  • Order spring pre-emergent early
  • Service and sharpen equipment
